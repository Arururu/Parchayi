CHIKANKARI

Chikankari, an esteemed traditional hand embroidery craft from Lucknow, India, holds a prominent place in artisanal heritage. With its roots tracing back to the Mughal era, Chikankari has thrived through the ages, embodying the amalgamation of Persian influences, Mughal patronage, and indigenous artistry. Passed down from one generation to the next, this craft has remained a symbol of artisanal heritage, carried forward by skilled hands that blend creativity and expertise. From delicate floral motifs to mesmerising patterns, Chikankari embroidery speaks volumes about the mastery of its artisans. The interplay of stitches like Bakhiya, Phanda, Tepchi, Rahet, and Keel Kangan adds depth and texture to the fabric, creating a symphony of intricacy that captivates the beholder.





Step into chikankari's captivating history, where each stitch whispers tales of beauty and perseverance. Though time may have veiled some garments, remnants of this rich heritage peek through, offering glimpses into bygone eras. Early to mid-19th century chikankari adorned elegant menswear, reflecting the grandeur of Mughal courts. More recent times saw the rise of graceful women's attire like dupattas and saris, embodying timeless charm. Historical pieces like the Dopalli topi (resembling mosque domes) and the Chaugoshia cap (favored by Lucknow's Nawabs) showcase the artistry. Garments like the Angarkha tunic and the Chapkan coat (a fusion of tradition and Western style) further illustrate chikankari's evolution. For women, the Shaluka blouse adds a touch of elegance, while dupattas and saris continue to captivate with their intricate embroidery.

The Debate: Bengali or Persian Origin?

Additionally, their openwork involves pulling threads. In contrast, Lucknowi chikankari is defined by its flowing floral motifs, delicate openwork patterns (jaali), and meandering stems. These jaali designs, a hallmark of chikankari, are achieved using a unique method that doesn't involve removing threads from the base fabric, unlike Persian techniques.

This technical distinction strengthens the argument for a Bengali origin for Lucknowi chikankari. While the exact origin remains a topic of discussion, some historical accounts may lack definitive proof, relying on folklore or local beliefs. Regardless, the primary and widely accepted narrative places Chikankari's roots in the Mughal era with a strong Persian influence. This treasured art form has left an indelible mark on Indian culture and fashion. It transcended being just an art form; for centuries, it has provided a livelihood for skilled artisans, particularly in Lucknow, Hyderabad, and Kolkata. These workshops became renowned for their distinctive interpretations and styles of chikankari embroidery.
